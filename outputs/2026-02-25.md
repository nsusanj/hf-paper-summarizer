Today, we look at several papers pushing the boundaries of what large models can *do* and *understand*. We have work on training LLMs to interact with terminals, efficient reranking techniques, new benchmarks for physical reasoning, and insights into how Test-Time Training actually works. A consistent theme is the challenge of moving from static perception to dynamic, interactive agency, and how data and architectural interpretations shape progress.

### Data Engineering for LLM Terminal Agents
https://huggingface.co/papers/2602.21193

This paper tackles the fundamental problem of how to effectively train LLMs for terminal interaction, a critical capability for software engineering agents. Current training data strategies for state-of-the-art terminal agents often lack transparency, making it difficult for researchers to iterate efficiently. The authors introduce **Terminal-Task-Gen**, a synthetic task generation pipeline that uses both seed-based and skill-based approaches to create diverse, executable terminal tasks. This pipeline yields **Terminal-Corpus**, a new open-source dataset. They also systematically investigate data engineering strategies like filtering, curriculum learning, and long context training.

Using Nemotron-Terminal models, fine-tuned from Qwen3 variants (8B, 14B, 32B), the authors demonstrate substantial gains on Terminal-Bench 2.0. For example, Nemotron-Terminal-32B improved from a baseline of 3.4% to 27.4% accuracy, notably outperforming a much larger Qwen3-Coder-480B model (23.9%). The performance improvements extended across challenging categories like Data Querying, Model Training, and Security, where base models often scored 0%. Interestingly, their study found that less strict filtering of generated trajectories—even keeping unsuccessful ones—proved more effective, likely exposing the model to valuable error states. Long context training offered no significant benefit, suggesting most high-quality supervision fits within standard windows.

This work provides a welcome contribution to the agentic AI landscape by open-sourcing a data generation pipeline and dataset. The results underscore that high-quality, targeted data engineering is more impactful than sheer model scale for acquiring specific agentic capabilities. The counter-intuitive findings on data filtering and long context use are particularly valuable insights for practitioners building terminal agents.

### Query-Focused Reranking via Attention Heads
https://huggingface.co/papers/2602.12192

Embedding models for retrieval suffer from a "geometric bottleneck," often failing to capture fine-grained query-document interactions. This paper introduces **QRRanker**, a lightweight listwise reranking framework that leverages attention scores from selected "Query-focused Retrieval (QR) heads" within an LLM to estimate passage-query relevance. Unlike traditional listwise rerankers that rely on Likert-scale generation, QRRanker produces continuous relevance scores, allowing training on any retrieval dataset.

QRRanker, built on a relatively small 4B parameter model, demonstrated strong performance, consistently outperforming state-of-the-art pointwise and listwise rerankers. On NarrativeQA, for instance, it achieved a Recall@10 of 54.93, significantly higher than GroupRank (48.83) and the vanilla QRHeads (48.89). It also translated to improved F1 scores on downstream QA tasks. The authors show that augmenting candidate passages with contextual summaries (e.g., event-centric for dialogue) further boosts accuracy on relevant benchmarks. Crucially, they found that training with attention heads from middle layers yielded comparable performance, enabling efficiency gains by truncating higher layers of the LLM during inference, leading to up to a 4x improvement in inference efficiency.

This is a clever and practical approach to reranking. By directly optimizing the intrinsic retrieval capabilities of LLM attention heads, QRRanker avoids the limitations of generative rerankers while maintaining a holistic view of the candidate list. The efficiency gains and strong performance make it a compelling alternative for large-scale long-context processing.

### Stabilizing RL for Agentic Multimodal Models
https://huggingface.co/papers/2602.20739

Reinforcement learning for agentic multimodal models often encounters "interaction collapse," where models learn to minimize tool usage and multi-turn reasoning, thereby limiting the benefits of agentic behavior. **PyVision-RL** proposes a new RL framework to stabilize training and sustain interaction in open-weight multimodal models.

The core of their approach involves an oversampling-filtering-ranking rollout strategy combined with an accumulative tool reward, specifically designed to prevent collapse and encourage sustained multi-turn tool use. For video reasoning, PyVision-Video, a model developed using this framework, incorporates "on-demand context construction," which selectively samples task-relevant frames during reasoning to reduce the number of visual tokens processed. While the full paper text is unavailable, the abstract claims "strong performance and improved efficiency," emphasizing that sustained interaction and efficient visual processing are crucial for developing scalable multimodal agents.

The described techniques for combating interaction collapse and managing visual context are directly relevant to building more capable and reliable multimodal agents. The focus on open-weight models is also important for broader research accessibility. Further details on the specific performance metrics and architectural choices would be beneficial for a deeper assessment.

### TTT with KV Binding: Not Memorization, But Linear Attention
https://huggingface.co/papers/2602.21204

This paper critically re-examines the common interpretation of Test-Time Training (TTT) with KV binding as a form of online meta-learning or memorization. The authors present empirical evidence that directly contradicts this view, showing TTT's behavior is inconsistent with a storage-and-retrieval mechanism.

Their empirical findings are quite striking: increasing inner-loop optimization steps, which should improve "memorization," actually *degrades* downstream task performance. Even more counter-intuitively, replacing gradient descent with *gradient ascent* in the inner loop maintains or sometimes improves performance. They also observe a significant distributional mismatch between queries and keys, and show that replacing queries with keys has a negligible effect on performance. These phenomena are incompatible with a memorization-based interpretation. The authors then analytically demonstrate that a broad class of TTT architectures, even those with complex inner loops like multi-layer MLPs and momentum, can be equivalently rewritten as a form of learned linear attention operator. This reframing resolves the empirical paradoxes and offers practical benefits: architectural simplifications (many components like weight normalization or momentum are often redundant), fully parallel formulations (achieving up to 4x inference throughput for the TTT layer), and a more generalized understanding of TTT.

This work offers a valuable re-evaluation of TTT. By shifting the understanding from "memorization" to "learned linear attention," the authors provide a more principled foundation for designing and optimizing these efficient sequence modeling layers. The practical implications, particularly the potential for simplification and parallelization, are significant for improving model efficiency.

### CHAIN: An Interactive Benchmark for Physical Reasoning in 3D
https://huggingface.co/papers/2602.21015

Existing Vision-Language Model (VLM) evaluations largely focus on static, single-turn tasks like VQA, which fail to assess an agent's ability to reason about physical constraints and plan actions in dynamic 3D environments. This paper introduces **CHAIN** (Causal Hierarchy of Actions and Interactions), an interactive 3D, physics-driven benchmark designed to evaluate how well models understand, plan, and execute structured action sequences grounded in physical constraints. Tasks include interlocking mechanical puzzles and 3D stacking/packing problems.

A comprehensive evaluation of state-of-the-art VLMs and diffusion-based models revealed significant limitations. Top-performing models, including GPT-5.2 and Kimi-k2.5, struggled to internalize physical structure and causal dependencies, often failing on long-horizon plans. Puzzle tasks proved especially challenging (most models achieving 0-3.1% success) compared to stacking (10.4-31.2%). "World models" (e.g., video generation models like Sora 2) exhibited catastrophic failures and severe hallucinations when asked to disassemble physical puzzles, routinely violating physics and struggling with object permanence. The study also highlighted that one-shot reasoning from static images performs uniformly worse than interactive approaches, underscoring the necessity of closed-loop feedback.

CHAIN is an important benchmark that moves VLM evaluation towards more realistic and challenging interactive physical reasoning. The results starkly illustrate the gap between current models' perceptual capabilities and their ability to plan and act in physically constrained environments. The insights into specific failure modes for puzzles (hidden constraints, first critical move) and stacking (object-set complexity, global spatial planning) are valuable for guiding future research in embodied AI and general-purpose agents.

### Compressing Multi-Vector Indices for Omni-Modal Retrieval
https://huggingface.co/papers/2602.21202

Multi-vector late interaction retrieval offers strong performance but suffers from linearly scaling storage and computation costs with document length, a critical issue for multimodal data (e.g., videos, visual documents). This work addresses this by proposing query-agnostic methods for compressing multi-vector document representations within a constant vector budget. They introduce **Attention-Guided Clustering (AGC)**, which uses learned universal query tokens to identify semantically salient regions, performs hard clustering, and then uses weighted aggregation for compression. They compare AGC to existing techniques like Sequence Resizing (SeqResize), Memory Tokens (MemTok), and Hierarchical Pooling (H-Pool).

Evaluated across text (BEIR), visual-document (ViDoRe), and video (MSR-VTT, MultiVENT 2.0) retrieval tasks, AGC consistently outperformed other parameterized methods and offered greater flexibility than non-parametric H-Pool. AGC often achieved competitive or even improved performance compared to a full, uncompressed index, setting a new state-of-the-art on MSR-VTT. This suggests that compression can sometimes reduce redundancy and noise in multimodal embeddings. Analysis of index utilization revealed that base models often underutilize their full document representation (e.g., ~1% active tokens), further justifying compression. AGC also demonstrated good transferability to unseen compression ratios and its performance correlated with a more even distribution of maximum similarity matches, indicating a better utilization of the compressed budget.

This paper provides highly practical solutions for scaling multimodal retrieval systems. The finding that aggressive compression can not only maintain but sometimes *improve* performance is a significant insight, challenging assumptions about information density in multimodal embeddings. AGC offers a robust and empirically validated approach to managing the computational burden of rich multimodal content, making late interaction more feasible for real-world applications.

Today's papers offered a blend of fundamental reinterpretations and practical advancements. The TTT paper provided a valuable theoretical re-anchoring, simplifying complex architectures. Simultaneously, works on terminal agents, rerankers, and multi-vector compression delivered concrete methods to improve agentic capabilities and system efficiency. The CHAIN benchmark served as a stark reminder of the long road ahead for true physical reasoning in LLMs. The persistent thread is the challenge of moving beyond static data processing to dynamic, interactive, and efficient agents, which will undoubtedly remain a key focus.
